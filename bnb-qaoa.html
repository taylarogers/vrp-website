Branch and Bound vs Quantum Approximate Optimisation Algorithm

Tayla Rogers

Research Questions

Points for the study to identify

  1. How does the Quantum Approximate Optimisation Algorithm (QAOA) perform compared to Branch and Bound (B&B) regarding solution quality, computational time, scalability, feasibility and success rate across various VRP problem sizes?
  2. Can enhancements in QAOA's optimiser (e.g.,COBYLA vs. SPSA) lead to performance improvements?
  3. What are the limitations of QAOA in solving VRP instances compared to B&B?

Algorithms

Brief explanations of both algorithms used in this study

Branch and Bound

B&B is an exact algorithm by nature, guaranteeing the discovery of the optimal solution given enough time and computational resources. It explores potential solutions in a structured tree-like manner as seen in the diagram below.

A branching strategy divides the problem into sub-problems represented by nodes in the tree. These sub-problems consist of deciding which customer to visit next in the current route. Bounding techniques eliminate the exploration for non-promising routes to improve performance. Selection rules are used to determine which sub-problem to explore next. The algorithm runs until all sub-problems have been explored or it reaches a timeout.

Quantum Approximate Optimisation Algorithm

Quantum Approximate Optimisation Algorithm (QAOA) is a hybrid quantum-classical algorithm, one of the most popular quantum approaches to solving combinatorial optimisation problems. The flow of how QAOA works can be seen in the diagram below.

The Mixer (HM) and Cost Hamiltonians (HC) are used together to balance exploration of the solution space and exploitation of problem information to drive the algorithm towards more optimal solutions. Penalty terms are added to HC to enforce problem constraints. The ansatz, a parameterised quantum circuit, is applied with the use of HM and HC to evolve the quantum state within the algorithm. The number of layers of the quantum circuit (p) balances the quality of the approximation with the complexity of the circuit. Parameters are fine-tuned by a classical optimiser to balance HM and HC.

Why Quantum Computing?

There is interest in the ability of quantum principles, namely entanglement and superposition, in solving complex combinatorial optimisation problems such as the VRP. IBM’s Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ) devices can use these principles to explore multiple routes concurrently. Hybrid quantum-classical methods are the current most practical implementation of quantum algorithms due to issues faced by the NISQ devices such as noise, errors and limited qubit counts.

Dataset

A customer dataset was created due to the scarcity of publicly available datasets. This includes both symmetric and asymmetric data inputs to evaluate algorithm versatility across multiple routing challenges.

The number of vehicles and destinations remains relatively small to stay within the practical limits of the QAOA and B&B algorithms. This includes problem sizes from 2 customers and 1 vehicle to 15 customers and 8 vehicles.

Performance Metrics

  • Solution quality: the total cost of the optimal route identified

  • CPU time: duration each algorithm takes to solve

  • Scalability: maximum problem size able to be solved

  • Feasibility: proportion of problem instances that meet all problem constraints

  • Success rate: how often solutions within certain thresholds of the best solution are produced

Experimental Settings

All experiments conducted on a classical computer, with the use of a quantum simulator to mimick the performance of quantum algorithms without needing access to a quantum computer. Specifications of computer can be found in the table below.

Each algorithm run 6 times per data input with a 6 hour timeout to manage computational time.
