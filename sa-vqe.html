Branch and Bound vs Quantum Approximate Optimisation Algorithm

Tayla Rogers

Research Questions

Points for the study to identify

  1. How does the Quantum Approximate Optimisation Algorithm (QAOA) perform compared to Branch and Bound (B&B) regarding solution quality, computational time, scalability, feasibility and success rate across various VRP problem sizes?
  2. Can enhancements in QAOA's optimiser (e.g.,COBYLA vs. SPSA) lead to performance improvements?
  3. What are the limitations of QAOA in solving VRP instances compared to B&B?

Algorithms

Brief explanations of both algorithms used in this study

Branch and Bound

B&B is an exact algorithm by nature, guaranteeing the discovery of the optimal solution given enough time and computational resources. It explores potential solutions in a structured tree-like manner as seen in the diagram below.

A branching strategy divides the problem into sub-problems represented by nodes in the tree. These sub-problems consist of deciding which customer to visit next in the current route. Bounding techniques eliminate the exploration for non-promising routes to improve performance. Selection rules are used to determine which sub-problem to explore next. The algorithm runs until all sub-problems have been explored or it reaches a timeout.

Quantum Approximate Optimisation Algorithm

Quantum Approximate Optimisation Algorithm (QAOA) is a hybrid quantum-classical algorithm, one of the most popular quantum approaches to solving combinatorial optimisation problems. The flow of how QAOA works can be seen in the diagram below.

The Mixer (HM) and Cost Hamiltonians (HC) are used together to balance exploration of the solution space and exploitation of problem information to drive the algorithm towards more optimal solutions. Penalty terms are added to HC to enforce problem constraints. The ansatz, a parameterised quantum circuit, is applied with the use of HM and HC to evolve the quantum state within the algorithm. The number of layers of the quantum circuit (p) balances the quality of the approximation with the complexity of the circuit. Parameters are fine-tuned by a classical optimiser to balance HM and HC.

Algorithmic Implementation

Implementation choices made in the construction of each algorithm

Branch and Bound

  • Branching strategy: generate all possible customer assignments to vehicles from the customers yet to be visited on the sub-route being looked at
  • Pruning method: upper-bound pruning used to eliminate partial solutions that exceed the best known cost at the time
  • Selection rule: Best-First Search (BFS) strategy used to prioritise sub-routes with the lowest current route cost

Quantum Approximate Optimisation Algorithm

  • p-value: value of 5 was selected to balance the speed and quality of results obtained to allow for a fair comparison across multiple metrics
  • Classical optimisers: both COBLYA and SPSA optimisers were used in implementation, as they have been found to produce the most accurate results when paired with QAOA

Results

Brief discussion on the experimental results obtained by each algorithm on the proposed performance metrics

Solution Quality

Due to its deterministic nature, B&B produced globally optimal results across all the problem sizes that it was able to solve.

Both QAOA variants performed well on smaller problem sizes, but as problem size increased their solution quality decreased. The SPSA optimiser generally offered routes closer to the optimal cost, however not significantly better than COBLYA. Neither optimiser allowed QAOA to be as effective as B&B. Both optimisers also produced an invalid solution in one problem size, choosing a solution with a lower optimal cost that did not follow the constraints of the problem. These invalid solutions can be seen on the above graph, where the QAOA algorithms obtained an optimal cost below that of B&B.

CPU Time

Considering the instances that were able to be solved, B&B maintained low computational time against most problem sizes. Negligible CPU time was observed for smaller instances, with a steady upward trend in times as problem size increased. B&B also showed consistent performance across most problem sizes, with its standard deviation only noticeably increasing as the problem size approached 10 customers.

QAOA produced significantly higher CPU times with the SPSA optimiser showing the sharpest increase in times. Even for smaller instances, SPSA required twice the amount of time than COBLYA. However, even COBLYA falls short of B&B’s performance. Both QAOA variants also showed a much higher variability in time compared to B&B. Despite the relatively higher standard deviation, it still remains small in comparison to the algorithm’s total computation time and do not noticeably affect the algorithm’s performance.

Scalability

From a dataset scaling up to 15 customers and 8 vehicles, B&B was able to solve up to a problem size of 15 customers and 1 vehicle given the available time and resource requirements. Its primary failure was it hitting 32GB memory limits on instances only involving 15 customers, highlighting its main weakness in its ability to solve real-world instances.

Both QAOA variants were only able to solve up to 4 customers and 3 vehicles, with it also being affected by memory limits. Above 6 customers, the algorithm reaches qubit limits. This limit is likely due to the many constraints necessary in the problem definition. This shows QAOA’s main limitation in its ability to solve combinatorial optimisation problems.

Feasibility

In the solvable instances, B&B consistently produced valid answers that follow all problem constraints. Both QAOA variants only produced feasible answers on 83.33% of instances, with both algorithms producing an invalid solution on one problem instance. These results occurred on a different problem size for each algorithm, implying different sensitivities to problem complexity between the optimisers. With its direct relationship, an increase in the algorithm’s p-value should raise this feasibility percentage, however it will also increase the computational time necessary.

Success Rate

The SPSA optimiser allowed QAOA a higher success rate on both 95% and 99% thresholds. However, it was still not able to match the accuracy of B&B. On the 99% thresholds, both optimisers produced lower success rates, highlighting the challenges faced by QAOA in obtaining highly accurate solutions.

Conclusions

Answers to the research questions that the insights of the study provided

1. How does QAOA perform compared to B&B regarding solution quality, computational time, scalability, feasibility and success rate across various VRP problem sizes?

B&B outperforms QAOA in solution quality, computational efficiency, scalability, feasibility, and success rate, especially as problem size increases. Competitiveness has been shown by QAOA on smaller problems, but a significant decline in performance has been observed with increased complexity. This indicates that further development is needed for QAOA to match classical methods in solving the VRP.

2. Can enhancements in QAOA’s optimiser lead to performance improvements?

Noticeable differences between the optimisers only emerge for instances larger than 3 customers and 2 vehicles. SPSA generally yielded solutions closer to the optimal cost, while COBYLA demonstrated lower computational times. However, neither optimiser matched B&B’s performance as problem size grew. This suggests that optimiser choice alone has is not enough to bridge the gap with classical methods.

3. What are the limitations of QAOA in solving VRP instances compared to B&B?

QAOA’s main limitations, compared to B&B, are highlighted by its lower feasibility percentage and reduced scalability. Current quantum limitations, such as qubit count, circuit depth, and noise levels, have forced constraints on QAOA, restricting its ability to solve larger problems effectively. A significant increase in computational time has also been observed with problem size, making QAOA less practical for larger, more complex VRP instances.
