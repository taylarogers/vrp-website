Simulated Annealing vs Variational Quantum Eigensolver

Ben Cleveland

Research Questions

Points for the study to identify

  1. How do variational quantum methods perform in terms of solution quality and computational time compared to metaheuristic methods across different problem instance sizes of the VRP?
  2. Is there a quantum advantage over classical methods for solving the VRP, and if so, under what conditions does this advantage manifest?
  3. For what range of problem instance sizes can the latest publicly available IBM quantum Qiskit algorithms and simulators produce solutions that are within 95% and 99% of the globally optimal solution?

Algorithms

Brief explanations of both algorithms used in this study

Simulated Annealing

Simulated Annealing (SA) is a metaheuristic algorithm inspired by the physical process of annealing in metallurgy. SA starts with an initial solution and iteratively explores the solution space by evaluating neighboring solutions. If a neighboring solution improves the current solution, it is accepted. If not, it may still be accepted based on a probability that decreases over time, allowing the algorithm to escape local optima and potentially find a global optimum.

The process is governed by a temperature parameter that decreases gradually, balancing exploration and exploitation. SA is widely used for solving combinatorial optimisation problems like the VRP due to its ability to find good solutions quickly across various problem sizes.

Variational Quantum Eigensolver

The Variational Quantum Eigensolver (VQE) is a hybrid quantum-classical algorithm designed to find the minimum eigenvalue of a Hamiltonian matrix, which corresponds to the optimal solution of the problem.

In VQE, a parameterized quantum circuit (ansatz) is prepared, and its parameters are iteratively adjusted by a classical optimiser to minimize the energy of the system. The quantum part calculates the energy for a given parameter set, while the classical optimiser updates the parameters based on this feedback. VQE has shown promise for solving combinatorial optimisation problems like the VRP but remains limited by current quantum hardware, such as the number of qubits and circuit depth.

Algorithmic Implementation

Implementation choices made in the construction of each algorithm

Simulated Annealing

  • Initial Solution: Generated using the savings algorithm, which produces more effective solutions than random generation.
  • Cooling Rate: Set to 0.99 to allow gradual cooling and thorough exploration of the solution space.
  • Stopping Criteria: Two variants tested — SA100 (100 stagnation limit) and SA1000 (1000 stagnation limit).
  • Objective Function: Minimize total vehicle distance while adhering to VRP constraints.
  • Temperature & Iterations: Initial temperature set to 100, with 100 iterations per temperature level.

Variational Quantum Eigensolver

  • Ansatz: Real Amplitudes ansatz chosen for its low circuit depth and efficiency.
  • Classical optimisers: COBYLA (linear approximations) and SPSA (stochastic gradient-based) were tested.
  • Qiskit Implementation: Used MinimumEigenOptimizer to convert VRP to an Ising Hamiltonian, with SamplingVQE managing computational load.

Results

Brief discussion on the experimental results obtained by each algorithm on the proposed performance metrics

Solution Quality

SA consistently produced high-quality solutions across all problem sizes, often reaching near-optimal solutions quickly. SA provided solutions within 87%–97% of the global optimum, with no invalid results observed. Due to its heuristic and stochastic nature, the solutions that are found vary, but are consistently near optimal cost and are produced quickly.

In contrast, VQE struggled as problem size increased. For smaller instances, VQE produced competitive solutions, particularly when using the SPSA optimiser, which performed slightly better than COBYLA. However, VQE's solution quality significantly degraded for larger problems, often failing to reach near-optimal solutions. Their scalability was limited, as touched on later.

CPU Time

SA demonstrated consistently low computational times across all problem sizes, completing computation in under a second, even for larger instances. This efficiency makes SA particularly suitable for real-world applications where rapid results are crucial.

On the other hand, VQE exhibited significantly higher CPU times, especially for larger problem instances. With the SPSA optimiser, VQE required notably more time than SA, with computational times ranging from 1 second for smaller instances to over 11,000 seconds for larger ones. The COBYLA optimiser performed even worse, frequently reaching the maximum allowed timeout of six hours on larger instances. The exponential increase in VQE’s CPU time after just the third instance highlights its scalability challenges compared to SA.

Scalability

SA scaled efficiently across all problem sizes, reliably solving instances from 2 customers and 1 vehicle up to 15 customers and 8 vehicles. SA's performance remained stable even as the problem size increased, making it a robust choice for larger, more complex VRP instances.

VQE, however, struggled significantly with scalability. While it could solve smaller instances effectively, its performance declined as the problem size grew. VQE with SPSA managed to solve up to 6 customer instances, but COBYLA reached its limit at 4 customers. The primary scalability bottlenecks for VQE were the memory limits for instances with 5 customers, and the qubit limits for instances with 6 or more customers, which severely limited its ability to handle larger problem sizes.

Feasibility

SA consistently produced 100% feasible solutions across all problem sizes, meaning that all generated solutions adhered to the constraints of the VRP, regardless of the complexity of the problem. In contrast, VQE with SPSA produced 1 infeasible solution out of the 6 solutions it was able to provide.

Success Rate

SA demonstrated high success rates, consistently achieving many solutions within 95% and 99% of the global optimum across all problem sizes. Success rates ranged between 87% and 97%, indicating that SA is highly reliable in generating near-optimal solutions, even for larger and more complex VRP instances. In contrast, the success rates of VQE were notably lower and more variable. For smaller problem sizes, VQE with SPSA performed relatively well, achieving success rates of 83% at both the 95% and 99% thresholds. However, as the problem size increased, VQE’s success rates diminished significantly, especially with the COBYLA optimiser, which only achieved 33% success within the same thresholds. The combination of quantum hardware limitations and optimiser performance led to a clear decline in success rates as problem complexity grew, highlighting VQE’s current limitations compared to SA.

Conclusions

Answers to the research questions that the insights of the study provided

1. How do variational quantum methods perform in terms of solution quality and computational time compared to metaheuristic methods across different problem instance sizes of the VRP?

VQE methods performed significantly worse than SA in both solution quality and computational time. While VQE with SPSA achieved success rates of 83% within 95%–99% of the global optimum for smaller instances, VQE’s performance degraded sharply as problem sizes increased. In contrast, SA consistently produced near-optimal solutions across all instances with success rates ranging from 87% to 97%, completing in just 0.2 seconds for most cases. VQE’s computation times spiked from 1 to 3 seconds for smaller instances and up to over 11,000 seconds for larger ones, with COBYLA frequently hitting timeouts. Overall, SA significantly outperformed VQE in both solution quality and computational efficiency.

2. Is there a quantum advantage over classical methods for solving the VRP, and if so, under what conditions does this advantage manifest?

No quantum advantage over classical methods, such as Simulated Annealing, was observed in this study. VQE struggled with scalability and failed to outperform SA in any meaningful way across various VRP instances. The limitations of current quantum simulators, including qubit count and computational overhead, prevented VQE from solving larger instances effectively. The results suggest that under present hardware and algorithmic constraints, quantum methods do not offer an advantage over classical metaheuristics for solving the VRP.

3. For what range of problem instance sizes can the latest publicly available IBM quantum Qiskit algorithms and simulators produce solutions that are within 95% and 99% of the globally optimal solution?

VQE with the SPSA optimiser achieved success rates of 83% at both the 95% and 99% thresholds for problem instances with up to 4 customers. VQE with COBYLA, on the other hand, achieved only 33% success within the same thresholds for instances with up to 3 customers. Beyond these sizes, success rates were not applicable due to inability to solve, indicating that current quantum algorithms struggle to maintain performance as problem complexity increases.
